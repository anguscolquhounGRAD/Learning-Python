1) Why am I doing this project?

Before coming to Thailand to train Muay Thai, I'd visited before and knew that, like much of Southeast Asia, the population was poor. What I wasn't prepared for was the scale of inequality in the country. Training really brought this home, it highlights just how deep the rural-urban divide is, and how Muay Thai works as one of the few mechanisms of social mobility for a lot of people. 

Once I started training, I quickly realised how difficult the challenge ahead of me was. A typical fighter's schedule looked like this: arrive at the gym for 6:30am for an hour-long group run, then training from 7:30 to 9am. The second session ran from 4 to 6pm. Training was Monday to Saturday, with Sunday as the only rest day. The volume, combined with training in 30-degree heat, took a massive toll on my body. Despite trying to condition myself before the trip, I couldn't keep up, and eventually started skipping the morning runs and 'only' training 10 times per week. 

The gyms I trained at had a mix of Westerners like me and Thai fighters, often aged anywhere from 10 to 19. The training intensity and volume make it pretty obvious why they're so young, most fighting careers are over by the time they hit their 20s. Despite the "always train more" culture, everyone slacks where they can. Thai or Western, if the coach said do 10 press-ups, you'd count people doing 7 when the coach was not looking.

I trained in the norther city of Chiang Mai, but talking to the Thai coaches, I'd ask where they were from and they'd often say 'Isaan', the rural east of the country. I'd ask how many fights they'd had and they'd say something like 300, starting from age 10. What I started to realise was that these coaches were the few who'd actually made it out of their rural towns and into the cities. Behind them was a huge number of kids who'd been ground down by the training along the way. And it's worth remembering these kids also had to go to school on top of training twice a day. It's almost accepted in Thai society that this is just how social mobility works — a bit like Squid Game, where only a handful make it through the toughest stages. Even prison doesn't escape this logic: Thailand is known for having Muay Thai gyms inside prisons, where inmates can fight for a shot at early release.

After coming home from my fight in Thailand, I wanted to keep my Python skills sharp from last year, but also dig into some of the insights I'd picked up about Thailand's economy while I was out there. So that's why I'm doing a data project on inequality in Thailand using Python.
